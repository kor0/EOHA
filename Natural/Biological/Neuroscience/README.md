# Neuroscience


<p><b>Neuroscience</b> (or <b>neurobiology</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">scientific study</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Merriam_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Multidisciplinary_approach" class="mw-redirect" title="Multidisciplinary approach">multidisciplinary</a> branch of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> that combines <a href="/wiki/Neurophysiology" title="Neurophysiology">physiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroanatomy" title="Neuroanatomy">anatomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_neuroscience" title="Molecular neuroscience">molecular biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Development_of_the_nervous_system" title="Development of the nervous system">developmental biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellular_neuroscience" title="Cellular neuroscience">cytology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_neuroscience" class="mw-redirect" title="Theoretical neuroscience">mathematical modeling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> to understand the fundamental and emergent properties of <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological neural network">neural circuits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The understanding of the biological basis of learning, memory, behavior, perception and consciousness has been described by <a href="/wiki/Eric_Kandel" title="Eric Kandel">Eric Kandel</a> as the "ultimate challenge" of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological Sciences">biological sciences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The scope of neuroscience has broadened over time to include different approaches used to study the nervous system at the different scales and the techniques used by <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">neuroscientists</a> have expanded enormously, from <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cell_biology" title="Cell biology">cellular</a> studies of individual neurons to <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">imaging</a> of sensory and motor tasks in the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>. Neuroscience has also given rise to such other disciplines as <a href="/wiki/Educational_neuroscience" title="Educational neuroscience">neuroeducation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Neuroethics" title="Neuroethics">neuroethics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neurolaw" title="Neurolaw">neurolaw</a>.
</p><p>As a result of the increasing number of scientists who study the nervous system, several prominent neuroscience organizations have been formed to provide a forum to all neuroscientists and educators. For example, the <a href="/wiki/International_Brain_Research_Organization" title="International Brain Research Organization">International Brain Research Organization</a> was founded in 1960,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Neurochemistry" title="International Society for Neurochemistry">International Society for Neurochemistry</a> in 1963,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/European_Brain_and_Behaviour_Society" title="European Brain and Behaviour Society">European Brain and Behaviour Society</a> in 1968,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Neuroscience" title="Society for Neuroscience">Society for Neuroscience</a> in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_neuroscience" title="History of neuroscience">History of neuroscience</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gray739.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Gray739.png/250px-Gray739.png" width="250" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Gray739.png/375px-Gray739.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Gray739.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="379" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gray739.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Illustration from <i><a href="/wiki/Gray%27s_Anatomy" title="Gray&#39;s Anatomy">Gray's Anatomy</a></i> (1918) of a lateral view of the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a>, featuring the <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a> among other neuroanatomical features</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest study of the nervous system dates to <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a>. <a href="/wiki/Trepanation" class="mw-redirect" title="Trepanation">Trepanation</a>, the surgical practice of either drilling or scraping a hole into the <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a> for the purpose of curing headaches or <a href="/wiki/Mental_illness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental illness">mental disorders</a>, or relieving cranial pressure, was first recorded during the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> period. Manuscripts dating to <a href="/wiki/1700_BC" class="mw-redirect" title="1700 BC">1700&#160;BC</a> indicate that the <a href="/wiki/Egyptians" title="Egyptians">Egyptians</a> had some knowledge about symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Brain_damage" title="Brain damage">brain damage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Early views on the function of the brain regarded it to be a "cranial stuffing" of sorts. In <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, from the late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Kingdom_of_Egypt" title="Middle Kingdom of Egypt">Middle Kingdom</a> onwards, the brain was regularly removed in preparation for <a href="/wiki/Mummy" title="Mummy">mummification</a>. It was believed at the time that the <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a> was the seat of intelligence. According to <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>, the first step of mummification was to "take a crooked piece of iron, and with it draw out the brain through the nostrils, thus getting rid of a portion, while the <a href="/wiki/Skull" title="Skull">skull</a> is cleared of the rest by rinsing with drugs."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The view that the heart was the source of consciousness was not challenged until the time of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_medicine" title="Ancient Greek medicine">Greek</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Hippocrates" title="Hippocrates">Hippocrates</a>. He believed that the brain was not only involved with sensation—since most specialized organs (e.g.,&#160;eyes, ears, tongue) are located in the head near the brain—but was also the seat of intelligence. <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">Plato</a> also speculated that the brain was the seat of the rational part of the soul.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, however, believed the heart was the center of intelligence and that the brain regulated the amount of heat from the heart.<sup id="cite_ref-Stanley2001_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stanley2001-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This view was generally accepted until the <a href="/wiki/Medicine_in_ancient_Rome" title="Medicine in ancient Rome">Roman</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Galen" title="Galen">Galen</a>, a follower of Hippocrates and physician to <a href="/wiki/Gladiator" title="Gladiator">Roman gladiators</a>, observed that his patients lost their mental faculties when they had sustained damage to their brains.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Qasim_al-Zahrawi" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu al-Qasim al-Zahrawi">Abulcasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">Averroes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Zuhr" title="Ibn Zuhr">Avenzoar</a>, and  <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>, active in the Medieval Muslim world, described a number of medical problems related to the brain. In <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vesalius" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesalius">Vesalius</a> (1514–1564), <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">René Descartes</a> (1596–1650), and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Willis" title="Thomas Willis">Thomas Willis</a> (1621–1675) also made several contributions to neuroscience.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg/220px-GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg/330px-GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6d/GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg 2x" data-file-width="409" data-file-height="272" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GolgiStainedPyramidalCell.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Golgi%27s_method" title="Golgi&#39;s method">Golgi stain</a> first allowed for the visualization of individual neurons.</div></div></div>
<p>In the first half of the 19th century, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Pierre_Flourens" title="Jean Pierre Flourens">Jean Pierre Flourens</a> pioneered the experimental method of carrying out localized lesions of the brain in living animals describing their effects on motricity, sensibility and behavior. Studies of the brain became more sophisticated after the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">microscope</a> and the development of a <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining procedure</a> by <a href="/wiki/Camillo_Golgi" title="Camillo Golgi">Camillo Golgi</a> during the late 1890s. The procedure used a <a href="/wiki/Silver_chromate" title="Silver chromate">silver chromate</a> salt to reveal the intricate structures of individual <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neurons</a>. His technique was used by <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal" title="Santiago Ramón y Cajal">Santiago Ramón y Cajal</a> and led to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Neuron_doctrine" title="Neuron doctrine">neuron doctrine</a>, the hypothesis that the functional unit of the brain is the neuron.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Golgi and Ramón y Cajal shared the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> in 1906 for their extensive observations, descriptions, and categorizations of neurons throughout the brain. While <a href="/wiki/Luigi_Galvani" title="Luigi Galvani">Luigi Galvani</a>'s pioneering work in the late 1700s had set the stage for studying the <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">electrical excitability</a> of muscles and neurons, it was in the late 19th century that <a href="/wiki/Emil_du_Bois-Reymond" title="Emil du Bois-Reymond">Emil du Bois-Reymond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Peter_M%C3%BCller" title="Johannes Peter Müller">Johannes Peter Müller</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hermann_von_Helmholtz" title="Hermann von Helmholtz">Hermann von Helmholtz</a> demonstrated that the electrical excitation of neurons predictably affected the electrical states of adjacent neurons,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Caton" title="Richard Caton">Richard Caton</a> found electrical phenomena in the cerebral hemispheres of rabbits and monkeys.
</p><p>In parallel with this research, work with brain-damaged patients by <a href="/wiki/Paul_Broca" title="Paul Broca">Paul Broca</a> suggested that certain regions of the brain were responsible for certain functions. At the time, Broca's findings were seen as a confirmation of <a href="/wiki/Franz_Joseph_Gall" title="Franz Joseph Gall">Franz Joseph Gall</a>'s theory that language was localized and that certain <a href="/wiki/Mental_process" title="Mental process">psychological functions</a> were localized in specific areas of the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Functional_specialization_(brain)" title="Functional specialization (brain)">localization of function</a> hypothesis was supported by observations of <a href="/wiki/Epilepsy" title="Epilepsy">epileptic</a> patients conducted by <a href="/wiki/John_Hughlings_Jackson" title="John Hughlings Jackson">John Hughlings Jackson</a>, who correctly inferred the organization of the <a href="/wiki/Motor_cortex" title="Motor cortex">motor cortex</a> by watching the progression of seizures through the body. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wernicke" title="Carl Wernicke">Carl Wernicke</a> further developed the theory of the specialization of specific brain structures in language comprehension and production. Modern research through <a href="/wiki/History_of_neuroimaging" title="History of neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a> techniques, still uses the <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Brodmann areas">Brodmann</a> <a href="/wiki/Cytoarchitectonics_of_the_cerebral_cortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Cytoarchitectonics of the cerebral cortex">cerebral cytoarchitectonic map</a> (referring to study of <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">cell structure</a>) anatomical definitions from this era in continuing to show that distinct areas of the cortex are activated in the execution of specific tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 20th century, neuroscience began to be recognized as a distinct academic discipline in its own right, rather than as studies of the nervous system within other disciplines. <a href="/wiki/Eric_Kandel" title="Eric Kandel">Eric Kandel</a> and collaborators have cited <a href="/wiki/David_Rioch" title="David Rioch">David Rioch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Francis_O._Schmitt" title="Francis O. Schmitt">Francis O. Schmitt</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Kuffler" title="Stephen Kuffler">Stephen Kuffler</a> as having played critical roles in establishing the field.<sup id="cite_ref-Rioch_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rioch-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Rioch originated the integration of basic anatomical and physiological research with clinical psychiatry at the <a href="/wiki/Walter_Reed_Army_Institute_of_Research" title="Walter Reed Army Institute of Research">Walter Reed Army Institute of Research</a>, starting in the 1950s. During the same period, Schmitt established a neuroscience research program within the Biology Department at the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>, bringing together biology, chemistry, physics, and mathematics. The first freestanding neuroscience department (then called Psychobiology) was founded in 1964 at the University of California, Irvine by <a href="/wiki/James_McGaugh" title="James McGaugh">James L. McGaugh</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  This was followed by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Neurobiology,_Harvard_Medical_School" title="Department of Neurobiology, Harvard Medical School">Department of Neurobiology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a> which was founded in 1966 by Stephen Kuffler.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The understanding of neurons and of nervous system function became increasingly precise and molecular during the 20th century. For example, in 1952, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lloyd_Hodgkin" title="Alan Lloyd Hodgkin">Alan Lloyd Hodgkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Huxley" title="Andrew Huxley">Andrew Huxley</a> presented a mathematical model for transmission of electrical signals in neurons of the giant axon of a squid, which they called "<a href="/wiki/Action_potentials" class="mw-redirect" title="Action potentials">action potentials</a>", and how they are initiated and propagated, known as the <a href="/wiki/Hodgkin%E2%80%93Huxley_model" title="Hodgkin–Huxley model">Hodgkin–Huxley model</a>. In 1961–1962, Richard FitzHugh and J. Nagumo simplified Hodgkin–Huxley, in what is called the <a href="/wiki/FitzHugh%E2%80%93Nagumo_model" title="FitzHugh–Nagumo model">FitzHugh–Nagumo model</a>. In 1962, <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Katz" title="Bernard Katz">Bernard Katz</a> modeled <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmission" title="Neurotransmission">neurotransmission</a> across the space between neurons known as <a href="/wiki/Synapses" class="mw-redirect" title="Synapses">synapses</a>. Beginning in 1966, Eric Kandel and collaborators examined biochemical changes in neurons associated with learning and memory storage in <i><a href="/wiki/Aplysia" title="Aplysia">Aplysia</a></i>. In 1981 Catherine Morris and Harold Lecar combined these models in the <a href="/wiki/Morris%E2%80%93Lecar_model" title="Morris–Lecar model">Morris–Lecar model</a>. Such increasingly quantitative work gave rise to numerous <a href="/wiki/Biological_neuron_model" title="Biological neuron model">biological neuron models</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_neuroscience">Modern neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_neuroscience" title="Outline of neuroscience">Outline of neuroscience</a></div> 
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg/290px-Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg.png" width="290" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg/435px-Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg/580px-Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="541" data-file-height="601" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nervous_system_diagram-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Human nervous system</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">scientific study</a> of the nervous system has increased significantly during the second half of the twentieth century, principally due to advances in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrophysiology" title="Electrophysiology">electrophysiology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Computational_neuroscience" title="Computational neuroscience">computational neuroscience</a>. This has allowed neuroscientists to study the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a> in all its aspects:  how it is structured, how it works, how it develops, how it malfunctions, and how it can be changed. For example, it has become possible to understand, in much detail, the complex processes occurring within a single <a href="/wiki/Neuron" title="Neuron">neuron</a>. Neurons are cells specialized for communication. They are able to communicate with neurons and other cell types through specialized junctions called <a href="/wiki/Synapse" title="Synapse">synapses</a>, at which electrical or electrochemical signals can be transmitted from one cell to another. Many neurons extrude a long thin filament of <a href="/wiki/Axoplasm" title="Axoplasm">axoplasm</a> called an <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axon</a>, which may extend to distant parts of the body and are capable of rapidly carrying electrical signals, influencing the activity of other neurons, muscles, or glands at their termination points. A nervous system emerges from the assemblage of neurons that are connected to each other.
</p><p>In vertebrates, the nervous system can be split into two parts, the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>), and the <a href="/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system" title="Peripheral nervous system">peripheral nervous system</a>. In many species — including all vertebrates — the nervous system is the most complex organ system in the body, with most of the complexity residing in the brain. The <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">human brain</a> alone contains around one hundred billion neurons and one hundred trillion synapses; it consists of thousands of distinguishable substructures, connected to each other in synaptic networks whose intricacies have only begun to be unraveled. The majority of the approximately 20,000–25,000 genes belonging to the human genome are expressed specifically in the brain. Due to the <a href="/wiki/Neuroplasticity" title="Neuroplasticity">plasticity</a> of the human brain, the structure of its synapses and their resulting functions change throughout life.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Thus the challenge of making sense of all this complexity is formidable.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Molecular_and_cellular_neuroscience">Molecular and cellular neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Molecular and cellular neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Molecular_neuroscience" title="Molecular neuroscience">Molecular neuroscience</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cellular_neuroscience" title="Cellular neuroscience">Cellular neuroscience</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Neuron_colored.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Neuron_colored.jpg/200px-Neuron_colored.jpg" width="200" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Neuron_colored.jpg/300px-Neuron_colored.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Neuron_colored.jpg/400px-Neuron_colored.jpg 2x" data-file-width="864" data-file-height="1013" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Neuron_colored.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photograph of a <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">stained neuron</a> in a chicken embryo</div></div></div>
<p>The study of the nervous system can be done at multiple levels, ranging from the molecular and cellular levels to the systems and cognitive levels. At the molecular level, the basic questions addressed in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_neuroscience" title="Molecular neuroscience">molecular neuroscience</a> include the mechanisms by which neurons express and respond to molecular signals and how <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> form complex connectivity patterns. At this level, tools from <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> are used to understand how neurons develop and how genetic changes affect biological functions. The <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphology</a>, molecular identity, and physiological characteristics of neurons and how they relate to different types of behavior are also of considerable interest.
</p><p>The fundamental questions addressed in <a href="/wiki/Cellular_neuroscience" title="Cellular neuroscience">cellular neuroscience</a> include the mechanisms of how neurons process <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">signals</a> physiologically and electrochemically. These questions include how signals are processed by neurites – thin extensions from a neuronal <a href="/wiki/Perikaryon" class="mw-redirect" title="Perikaryon">cell body</a>, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Dendrite" title="Dendrite">dendrites</a> (specialized to receive synaptic inputs from other neurons) and <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">axons</a> (specialized to conduct nerve impulses called <a href="/wiki/Action_potential" title="Action potential">action potentials</a>) – and somas (the cell bodies of the neurons containing the nucleus), and how <a href="/wiki/Neurotransmitter" title="Neurotransmitter">neurotransmitters</a> and electrical signals are used to process information in a neuron. Another major area of neuroscience is directed at investigations of the <a href="/wiki/Neural_development" class="mw-redirect" title="Neural development">development</a> of the nervous system. These questions include the <a href="/wiki/Regional_specification" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional specification">patterning and regionalization</a> of the nervous system, neural <a href="/wiki/Stem_cell" title="Stem cell">stem cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cellular_differentiation" title="Cellular differentiation">differentiation</a> of neurons and glia, <a href="/wiki/Neuronal_migration" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuronal migration">neuronal migration</a>, axonal and dendritic development, <a href="/wiki/Growth_factor" title="Growth factor">trophic interactions</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Synaptogenesis" title="Synaptogenesis">synapse formation</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Computational_neurogenetic_modeling" title="Computational neurogenetic modeling">Computational neurogenetic modeling</a> is concerned with the development of dynamic neuronal models for modeling brain functions with respect to genes and dynamic interactions between genes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neural_circuits_and_systems">Neural circuits and systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neural circuits and systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Biological_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological neural network">Biological neural network</a> and <a href="/wiki/Systems_neuroscience" title="Systems neuroscience">Systems neuroscience</a></div>
<p>At the systems level, the questions addressed in <a href="/wiki/Systems_neuroscience" title="Systems neuroscience">systems neuroscience</a> include how <a href="/wiki/Biological_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological neural network">biological neural networks</a> or neural circuits are formed and used anatomically and physiologically to produce functions such as <a href="/wiki/Reflex" title="Reflex">reflexes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multisensory_integration" title="Multisensory integration">multisensory integration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motor_coordination" title="Motor coordination">motor coordination</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circadian_rhythm" title="Circadian rhythm">circadian rhythms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotional responses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>. In other words, they address how these neural circuits function and the mechanisms through which behaviors are generated. For example, systems level analysis addresses questions concerning specific sensory and motor modalities: how does <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">vision</a> work? How do <a href="/wiki/Songbirds" class="mw-redirect" title="Songbirds">songbirds</a> learn new songs and <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a> localize with <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasound</a>? How does the <a href="/wiki/Somatosensory_system" title="Somatosensory system">somatosensory system</a> process tactile information? The related fields of <a href="/wiki/Neuroethology" title="Neuroethology">neuroethology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neuropsychology" title="Neuropsychology">neuropsychology</a> address the question of how neural substrates underlie specific <a href="/wiki/Ethology" title="Ethology">animal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_behavior" title="Human behavior">human</a> behaviors. <a href="/wiki/Neuroendocrinology" title="Neuroendocrinology">Neuroendocrinology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychoneuroimmunology" title="Psychoneuroimmunology">psychoneuroimmunology</a> examine interactions between the nervous system and the <a href="/wiki/Endocrinology" title="Endocrinology">endocrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Immunology" title="Immunology">immune</a> systems, respectively. Despite many advancements, the way that networks of neurons perform complex <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive processes</a> and behaviors is still poorly understood.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognitive_and_behavioral_neuroscience">Cognitive and behavioral neuroscience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cognitive and behavioral neuroscience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_neuroscience" title="Cognitive neuroscience">Cognitive neuroscience</a></div>
<p>At the cognitive level, <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_neuroscience" title="Cognitive neuroscience">cognitive neuroscience</a> addresses the questions of how <a href="/wiki/Mental_process" title="Mental process">psychological functions</a> are produced by <a href="/wiki/Biological_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological neural network">neural circuitry</a>. The emergence of powerful new measurement techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/FMRI" class="mw-redirect" title="FMRI">fMRI</a>, <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">PET</a>, <a href="/wiki/SPECT" class="mw-redirect" title="SPECT">SPECT</a>), <a href="/wiki/Electrophysiology" title="Electrophysiology">electrophysiology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genetic analysis</a> combined with sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Experimental_techniques" class="mw-redirect" title="Experimental techniques">experimental techniques</a> from <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a> allows <a href="/wiki/Neuroscientist" title="Neuroscientist">neuroscientists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologists</a> to address abstract questions such as how human cognition and emotion are mapped to specific neural substrates. Although many studies still hold a reductionist stance looking for the neurobiological basis of cognitive phenomena, recent research shows that there is an interesting interplay between neuroscientific findings and conceptual research, soliciting and integrating both perspectives. For example, the neuroscience research on empathy solicited an interesting interdisciplinary debate involving philosophy, psychology and psychopathology.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, the neuroscientific identification of multiple memory systems related to different brain areas has challenged the idea of memory as a literal reproduction of the past, supporting a view of memory as a generative, constructive and dynamic process.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Neuroscience is also allied with the <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social</a> and <a href="/wiki/Behavioral_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Behavioral sciences">behavioral sciences</a> as well as nascent interdisciplinary fields such as <a href="/wiki/Neuroeconomics" title="Neuroeconomics">neuroeconomics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">decision theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_neuroscience" title="Social neuroscience">social neuroscience</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neuromarketing" title="Neuromarketing">neuromarketing</a> to address complex questions about interactions of the brain with its environment. A study into consumer responses for example uses <a href="/wiki/Electroencephalography" title="Electroencephalography">EEG</a> to investigate neural correlates associated with <a href="/wiki/Transportation_theory_(psychology)" title="Transportation theory (psychology)">narrative transportation</a> into stories about <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ultimately neuroscientists would like to understand every aspect of the nervous system, including how it works, how it develops, how it malfunctions, and how it can be altered or repaired. The specific topics that form the main foci of research change over time, driven by an ever-expanding base of knowledge and the availability of increasingly sophisticated technical methods. Over the long term, improvements in technology have been the primary drivers of progress. Developments in electron microscopy, computers, electronics, functional brain imaging, and most recently genetics and genomics, have all been major drivers of progress.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">Neurology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurosurgery" title="Neurosurgery">neurosurgery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psychosurgery" title="Psychosurgery">psychosurgery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anesthesiology" title="Anesthesiology">anesthesiology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pain_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Pain medicine">pain medicine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuropathology" title="Neuropathology">neuropathology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroradiology" title="Neuroradiology">neuroradiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmology" title="Ophthalmology">ophthalmology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otolaryngology" class="mw-redirect" title="Otolaryngology">otolaryngology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinical_neurophysiology" title="Clinical neurophysiology">clinical neurophysiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Addiction_medicine" title="Addiction medicine">addiction medicine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sleep_medicine" title="Sleep medicine">sleep medicine</a> are some medical specialties that specifically address the diseases of the nervous system. These terms also refer to clinical disciplines involving diagnosis and treatment of these diseases. Neurology works with diseases of the central and peripheral nervous systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a> (ALS) and <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a>, and their medical treatment. Psychiatry focuses on <a href="/wiki/Affect_(psychology)" title="Affect (psychology)">affective</a>, behavioral, <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceptual</a> disorders. Anesthesiology focuses on perception of pain, and pharmacologic alteration of consciousness. Neuropathology focuses upon the classification and underlying pathogenic mechanisms of central and peripheral nervous system and muscle diseases, with an emphasis on morphologic, microscopic, and chemically observable alterations. Neurosurgery and psychosurgery work primarily with surgical treatment of diseases of the central and peripheral nervous systems. The boundaries between these specialties have been blurring recently as they are all influenced by <a href="/wiki/Basic_research" title="Basic research">basic research</a> in neuroscience. <a href="/wiki/Brain_imaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Brain imaging">Brain imaging</a> also enables objective, biological insights into mental illness, which can lead to faster diagnosis, more accurate prognosis, and help assess patient progress over time.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Integrative_neuroscience" title="Integrative neuroscience">Integrative neuroscience</a> makes connections across these specialized areas of focus.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neuroscience_organizations">Neuroscience organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Neuroscience organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The largest professional neuroscience organization is the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Neuroscience" title="Society for Neuroscience">Society for Neuroscience</a> (SFN), which is based in the United States but includes many members from other countries.  Since its founding in 1969 the SFN has grown steadily:  as of 2010 it recorded 40,290 members from 83 different countries.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  Annual meetings, held each year in a different American city, draw attendance from researchers, postdoctoral fellows, graduate students, and undergraduates, as well as educational institutions, funding agencies, publishers, and hundreds of businesses that supply products used in research.
</p><p>Other major organizations devoted to neuroscience include the <a href="/wiki/International_Brain_Research_Organization" title="International Brain Research Organization">International Brain Research Organization</a> (IBRO), which holds its meetings in a country from a different part of the world each year, and the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_European_Neuroscience_Societies" title="Federation of European Neuroscience Societies">Federation of European Neuroscience Societies</a> (FENS), which holds a meeting in a different European city every two years.  FENS comprises a set of 32 national-level organizations, including the <a href="/wiki/British_Neuroscience_Association" title="British Neuroscience Association">British Neuroscience Association</a>, the German Neuroscience Society (<i>Neurowissenschaftliche Gesellschaft</i>), and the French <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.neurosciences.asso.fr/">Société des Neurosciences</a></i>. The first National Honor Society in Neuroscience, <a href="/wiki/Nu_Rho_Psi" title="Nu Rho Psi">Nu Rho Psi</a>, was founded in 2006.
</p><p>In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/BRAIN_Initiative" title="BRAIN Initiative">BRAIN Initiative</a> was announced in the US.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_education_and_outreach">Public education and outreach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neuroscience&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Public education and outreach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to conducting traditional research in laboratory settings, neuroscientists have also been involved in the <a href="/wiki/Public_awareness_of_science" title="Public awareness of science">promotion of awareness and knowledge</a> about the nervous system among the general public and government officials. Such promotions have been done by both individual neuroscientists and large organizations. For example, individual neuroscientists have promoted neuroscience education among young students by organizing the <a href="/wiki/International_Brain_Bee" title="International Brain Bee">International Brain Bee</a>, which is an academic competition for high school or secondary school students worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, large organizations such as the Society for Neuroscience have promoted neuroscience education by developing a primer called Brain Facts,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> collaborating with public school teachers to develop Neuroscience Core Concepts for K-12 teachers and students,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and cosponsoring a campaign with the <a href="/wiki/Dana_Foundation" title="Dana Foundation">Dana Foundation</a> called Brain Awareness Week to increase public awareness about the progress and benefits of brain research.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In Canada, the CIHR Canadian National Brain Bee is held annually at <a href="/wiki/McMaster_University" title="McMaster University">McMaster University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Finally, neuroscientists have also collaborated with other education experts to study and refine educational techniques to optimize learning among students, an emerging field called <a href="/wiki/Educational_neuroscience" title="Educational neuroscience">educational neuroscience</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Federal agencies in the United States, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="National Institute of Health">National Institute of Health</a> (NIH)<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/National_Science_Foundation" title="National Science Foundation">National Science Foundation</a> (NSF),<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> have also funded research that pertains to best practices in teaching and learning of neuroscience concepts.
</p>
